## Response 1 — Naming & Registering

Here's a practical overview for a BCBA in Illinois who wants to start a private-pay IEP advocacy business (not ABA therapy, not billing insurance).

**1. PLLC vs LLC for a BCBA-owned advocacy practice in Illinois.** Illinois allows PLLCs for certain licensed professions (physicians, psychologists, some mental-health professions). The Behavior Analyst Licensing Act (225 ILCS 6) and IDFPR guidance focus on when a BCBA must hold an Illinois LBA license, and that licensure is required regardless of reimbursement source if providing/supervising behavior analysis services in Illinois [2]. There is no clear publicly available Illinois authority specifically stating BCBAs must use a PLLC rather than a standard LLC. For a non-clinical IEP advocacy practice (no direct ABA, no insurance billing), the work arguably falls outside "behavior analysis services" as defined in the Act and is consulting/advocacy, which is typically not regulated as a professional service the way clinical practice is. Practical implication (verify with counsel): a regular LLC is almost certainly available for an advocacy/consulting practice; a PLLC is unlikely to be required for non-clinical advocacy.

**2. IL SOS business name rules and designators.** The name must be "distinguishable upon the records" from existing entities — names differing only by punctuation, designator, or "the/a/an" typically won't qualify. Availability check: apps.ilsos.gov/corporatellc/. Optional name reservation holds the name for ~90 days. Required designators: "Limited Liability Company," "LLC," or "L.L.C." (PLLC equivalents if applicable). Cannot use "Inc.," "Corp.," "Ltd." in an LLC name. Restricted words ("bank," "insurance," "engineering," "architect") need additional approvals. "IEP" and "advocacy" are normally acceptable.

**3. Filing process and timing.** (a) Verify name; (b) decide registered agent (must have IL street address; can be yourself); (c) file Articles of Organization online at apps.ilsos.gov; (d) wait for file-stamped Articles; (e) apply for EIN at irs.gov (free, instant); (f) draft Operating Agreement (not filed but recommended); (g) register locally if required; (h) get professional liability insurance. Online LLC filings typically process within a few business days; paper filings can take several weeks.

**4. BCBA license implications.** If providing behavior analysis services in IL, an LBA license is required. If the practice is limited to IEP/504 meeting support, records review, drafting parent concerns, explaining IDEA/FAPE/LRE, suggesting questions — that's education advocacy/consultation, which is not a licensed profession in IL. No public IL rule bans use of "BCBA" in personal credentials. Take care that the entity name doesn't suggest a licensed clinical practice if not providing clinical services.

**5. Sole prop vs LLC.** Sole proprietorship: automatic, simple, no legal separation, unlimited personal liability [12]. LLC: separate legal entity, limited personal liability, default pass-through taxation similar to sole prop for single-member LLCs [12]. For an advocate giving advice that affects children's education and may be second-guessing district decisions, most risk-aware advisors recommend LLC over sole prop for asset protection and clearer business/personal boundary. Still need a well-worded client agreement and professional liability insurance tailored to education advocacy/consulting.

## Response 2 — Liability Insurance

**1. Will a typical BCBA malpractice policy cover non-clinical IEP advocacy?** BCBA-marketed policies (CM&F, CPH, HPSO) are explicitly written around behavior analysis/health-care services. CPH notes that BCBAs "work directly with students and their families" and recommends professional liability insurance for protection; independent contractors "will likely not be covered under an employer's policy" [1]. CM&F markets BCBA liability with limits like $1M per claim / $4M aggregate covering schools, clinics, in-home, and remote services [9]. These policies assume ABA or clinical/behavioral services, not standalone educational advocacy. Most forms cover "professional services" rendered "as a behavior analyst," and non-clinical IEP advocacy can fall outside that scope. Many insurers will treat IEP advocacy as education consulting / special education advocacy rather than health-care malpractice. Do not assume automatic coverage. Coverage depends on the professional capacity listed on the declarations, the definition of professional services, and any endorsements/exclusions. Ask the BCBA carrier in writing whether non-clinical advocacy is covered; request endorsements if "yes," obtain a separate E&O if "no."

**2. Carriers for special-education / IEP advocates.** This niche is usually insured as professional liability / E&O under titles like "educational consultant," "special education advocate," "non-lawyer advocacy services." Routes: broker/aggregator platforms (SimplyBusiness, Hiscox via brokers, Next, biBERK); traditional commercial carriers (The Hartford, State Farm, Travelers, CNA, Hiscox) writing professional liability under miscellaneous programs [8][13]; specialized professional liability brokers placing educational consultants and non-attorney disability advocates. Named BCBA-focused carriers (HPSO, CPH, CM&F) do not publicly market "special education advocate" as a stand-alone class — some will add a consulting endorsement, others insist on a separate policy. Best route: contact a local independent commercial broker in Illinois and ask specifically for E&O for a non-attorney special education advocate / educational consultant.

**3. Scope of services to disclose.** Records review (educational, psychological, related records for non-legal advocacy); IEP/504 meeting participation (non-attorney parent advocate/consultant, no legal advice/representation); school observations (non-clinical, with parental and district permission); mediation/dispute-resolution support (non-attorney, helping articulate concerns and goals); expert opinions / written reports (regarding educational needs, IEP/504 adequacy, services/placement, ESY); boundaries you maintain (no legal services, no due-process petitions, no outcome guarantees, no funds management). Also disclose credentials (BCBA, IL LBA if applicable), whether clinical ABA is also offered in private practice, and whether expert witness testimony is part of scope.

**4. Typical limits and premium ranges.** Common limits: $1M/$1M, $1M/$2M, or $1M/$3-4M (CM&F BCBA policy up to $1M/$4M [9]). No statutory IL minimum for advocates. Many solo advocates start at $1M/$1M or $1M/$2M. Premiums (very rough): BCBA clinical individual policies often $100-$300/year for basic limits (CPH starting near $100/year [1]); miscellaneous professional liability / E&O for solo consultant frequently $300-$800/year for $1M/$1M or $1M/$2M; higher with expert testimony or contentious scope.

**5. Employer-provided coverage extension.** Strongly warn against assuming employer coverage protects outside work. CPH: independent contractors "likely not covered" under employer policy [1]. AAPA: have a personal liability policy portable from job to job [2]. CM&F: employer policies structured to protect institutions first, may not cover licensure defense or post-employment claims [4]. Public school district / cooperative coverage is typically a public entity liability policy or educators' legal liability covering employees acting within scope of employment — almost never covers private-pay families outside employment, separate business entity work, or scope outside defined job duties. Default assumption: employer-provided coverage will not extend to private practice. Confirm in writing with the district risk office.

## Response 3 — Services and Fee Structure

**1. Core services catalog.** Initial consultation/case intake (45-60 min phone/video; free 15-20 min discovery + paid 60-min intake at hourly or flat); records review (hourly in 0.1-0.25 increments, or flat tiers by document volume; deliverables: written issue summary); IEP strategy/preparation (review current IEP, draft parent concerns letter, identify needed evaluations/services, propose goals; hourly or flat "IEP Prep Package"); IEP meeting attendance (pre-meeting brief, attend, real-time notes, debrief; hourly with 2-hour minimum or flat per meeting; written summary deliverable); school observation (observe student across settings; flat fee or hourly including travel and report); follow-up correspondence/advocacy (drafting parent emails/letters, reviewing district drafts; hourly with micro-task increments or support bundle); mediation/due process support — non-legal (educate parent, organize documentation/timeline, draft issue list, attend as support person; hourly with higher "contested matter" rate); expert opinion/consultation — BCBA-specific (FBA/BIP review, written critique, ABA best practice grounding; higher hourly or flat per review).

**2. Pricing models.** Hourly billing — flexible, matches actual time; base $125-$250+ depending on region/experience; minimums for meetings/travel; bill for prep, meetings, calls, emails, drafting, travel where allowed. Flat-fee packages — best for clearly defined limited scopes (IEP Review & Strategy, IEP Meeting Support); predictable but scope-creep risk. Retainers — classic trust ($1,000-$3,000 upfront billed against hourly) or monthly access (monthly fee for availability + set hours); best for ongoing complex cases. Most solo advocates use a hybrid: hourly baseline + flat-fee packages + retainer for high-need families.

**3. Market hourly rates.** A parent-advocacy directory cites $100-$300/hr for independent advocates in Illinois [no specific citation #]. Broader US: smaller/rural $75-$150; mid-sized cities/suburbs $125-$225; major metros and highly experienced/dual-credential $175-$350+. Suburban Illinois/Chicago: entry-to-mid $125-$175; experienced/specialized (BCBA with strong reputation) $175-$250+. Pricing considerations: years as BCBA, niche (ASD, behaviorally complex), target client income, accessible vs premium positioning.

**4. BCBA credential effect on pricing.** Justifies higher end of market due to: deep behavioral expertise (interpret FBAs, BIPs, progress data); clinical/educational credibility (schools used to working with BCBAs; speaks both languages); high demand niche (behavior/autism cases common in advocacy; many advocates lack strong behavior training); risk/complexity (behavior plans, restraint/seclusion, exclusionary discipline). Practical: base advocacy rate $150-$185/hr starting + BCBA expert consultation rate $185-$225/hr for ABA-grounded tasks; OR single rate at upper end of local advocacy market.

**5. Scope-of-engagement boundaries.** No legal representation / no legal advice — IL AG guidance distinguishes lay advocates from attorneys and notes advocates "cannot substitute [their] judgement for yours" [1]; advocate does not provide legal opinions, file pleadings, or act as legal representative; can educate on rights, organize facts, help formulate questions. No clinical diagnosis or treatment — separate advocacy from clinical: no diagnoses, no ongoing ABA treatment under this engagement, no medical/mental-health determinations; can interpret existing reports and offer professional opinion on adequacy of behavior supports. No insurance billing — advocacy is educational consultation, not medically-necessary ABA therapy, generally not covered by health insurance; private-pay only with clear written fee schedule. No school-directed services — advocate does not direct school staff clinically or implement behavior plans as a provider. Other typical exclusions: no transportation/childcare, no guaranteed outcomes, no non-IEP legal matters.

## Response 4 — Backend Systems

**Overview.** A solo advocacy backend needs to: store sensitive student records securely; organize each client's file; track contracts and signatures; manage appointments and intake; support a sensible retention schedule; enable secure deletion at end-of-life. IEP advocacy involves student education records — FERPA-sensitive handling even though you are not a school.

**1. Cloud storage (Google Workspace Business vs Microsoft 365 Business).** FERPA prescribes no specific vendor — both work if configured carefully. AWS FERPA guidance emphasizes access restrictions, security controls, and the school-official/authorized-use model [9]. Priorities for either platform: strong authentication (MFA on every account); role-based access control; restricted sharing (limit links, external sharing, domain-wide defaults); encryption at rest and in transit; audit logs / activity history; recovery and backup controls; easy offboarding. Google Workspace Business: familiar UI, easy folder/share-link workflow, strong admin controls in higher tiers; watch for open link-sharing risks and permission mess from individual file sharing. Microsoft 365 Business: strong enterprise controls, document-centric workflows, SharePoint/OneDrive permissions more structured; complexity for solo operators. For a solo practice the best choice is whichever can be reliably enforced for MFA, restricted sharing, folder permissions, auditability, retention.

**2. Folder structure.** Top-level: 00_Admin, 01_Intake, 02_Active_Clients, 03_Forms_and_Templates, 04_Contracts, 05_Billing, 06_Marketing_and_Website, 07_Archive, 08_Retention_Deletion_Log. Per-client folder (Lastname_Firstname): 00_Active_Summary, 01_Intake_and_Consent, 02_Evaluations_and_Reports, 03_IEP_and_504_Documents, 04_School_Communications, 05_Meeting_Notes, 06_Action_Items, 07_Drafts_and_Work_Product, 08_Final_Deliverables, 09_Invoice_and_Contract, 10_Archive. Naming convention: YYYY-MM-DD_DocumentType_ClientName_Version. Keep source records separate from work product; final PDFs in final deliverables folder; one master index spreadsheet for case tracking.

**3. Retention policy.** FERPA does not set a fixed retention period [1][13]. Common baseline: 7 years after case closure, aligned with civil-record retention, tax/accounting practices, contract-dispute defense, malpractice/risk management, and continuity. Longer retention when: state law requires; contract promises longer; legal dispute anticipated; record tied to ongoing matter; tax/accounting requirements; family asks per a written policy. Active case records: keep throughout engagement. Closed case files: 7 years after closure. Financial/tax records: per accounting/tax requirements. Legal holds: suspend deletion if dispute/complaint/claim reasonably expected.

**4. E-signature tools.** Google native — lightweight; good for very simple internal forms; less ideal for polished client contracts. DocuSign — industry standard, strong audit trail, good templates and reminders, familiar to clients, often more expensive. PandaDoc — excellent for proposals + service agreements, template-driven, polished client-facing, may be more feature-rich than needed. HelloSign/Dropbox Sign — simple, clean, often a good fit for straightforward agreements. For a solo practice, HelloSign/Dropbox Sign or DocuSign are cleanest. Must-have features: reusable services agreement template, automatic copy to storage, reminder emails, PDF archiving, audit log retention.

**5. Scheduling integration.** Calendly: simple, client-friendly, embeddable, booking page, intake questions, buffers, auto-confirmations/reminders, integration with Google/Microsoft calendar [11]. Google Appointments: native Workspace integration, simple setup, fewer third-party tools. Best practice: scheduling is the first step of intake — client books discovery call, booking form collects minimal info, auto-welcome email, then routed to services agreement + consent + intake questionnaire + secure upload. Don't collect too much sensitive info in the scheduler itself. Don't allow bookings before contract signature if policy requires.

**6. Secure deletion.** "Delete" can mean removing from visible folder, deleting from trash, or ensuring not recoverable from backups or key material. Cloud sanitization commonly points to clear/purge/destroy; for encrypted data, crypto-shredding (key destruction) is practical [4][8][12]. Practical steps at end of retention: confirm trigger; check for legal hold; delete active files (move to trash and empty); purge duplicates (synced folders, shared copies, exports, downloads, email attachments); address backups (document retention cycle; if can't selectively remove, ensure backup ages out per policy); if encrypted, use crypto-shredding; document the deletion (filename, client, date, method, locations cleared, backup/hold notes). Don't rely on recycle bin alone or assume "delete" means unrecoverable.

## Response 5 — Intake Process

**0. Big picture.** Inquiry → Discovery/Fit Call → post-call follow-up → Intake Form + Document Request → Welcome Packet + Services Agreement → Signature & Payment Setup → Onboarding → Records Review → First Paid Session/Meeting Prep. At each step: clarify fit, set expectations, protect yourself legally and ethically.

**1. Discovery / fit call.** Purpose: determine fit (needs match scope/skills, no conflicts of interest, capacity to take on); give parents realistic picture of what advocates can/can't do; decide on next steps (proceed or refer out). Length: 20-30 minutes by phone or secure video. Outline: introductions; parent's high-level concern (2-5 min); clarifying questions (student, district, IEP/504 status, deadlines); explanation of role/process/fees; conflict/scope/capacity screening; clear yes/no/next step. Most solo advocates offer a short free discovery call as marketing + screening; paid time for document review, strategy, IEP goal drafting. Screen for: conflicts of interest (prior advocate/attorney, district representation, personal relationships); scope fit (issue type — eligibility, IEP development, placement, behavior/discipline, manifestation, ESY, transition, due process; status — pre-referral through hearing; jurisdiction; complexity); capacity/timeline (upcoming deadlines, ideal timeline, expected cadence).

**2. Intake form: content and secure collection.** Family & contact (parent/guardian names, pronouns optional, relationship, address, phone, email, preferred contact, best times). Student basics (name, pronouns optional, DOB, current grade, school/district, placement, primary language, interpreter need). Presenting concerns (top 1-3, 6-12 month goals). Current supports & status (IEP/504 yes/no/not sure; current diagnoses; current services; upcoming meetings; pending complaint/mediation/due process). History snapshot (when concerns arose, major events, removals/suspensions, hospitalizations). Records to gather checklist (most recent IEP + last 1-2 prior, evaluation reports school/private/medical, 504 plan, progress reports/report cards last 12-24 months, BIP/behavior incident reports/suspension letters, optional medical/mental health with release). Referral source. Secure collection: encrypted form/portal with BAA where relevant; avoid email attachments for sensitive data; provide secure upload link or client portal; password-protect shared folders with passwords sent separately.

**3. Welcome packet.** Role & what you do (educational advocate, not attorney; review records, explain IEPs/evaluations, draft parent input, attend meetings as support/advocate, collaborate with related professionals with permission). What you do not do (legal advice/representation, guarantee outcomes, communicate with school without consent, clinical diagnosis/treatment, work outside geographic/scope limits). Modality & logistics (meeting format, in-person vs virtual school meeting attendance, late arrival/no-show policy). Communication norms (preferred channels — email logistics, secure portal documents; response times — e.g., 2 business days; urgent flagging; days/hours available; nights/weekends/holidays handling). Fees & billing summary (rate structure, billable time definition, billing cycle, payment methods). Client responsibilities (accurate info, share docs 3-5 business days before meetings, prompt notification of meetings/deadlines, early communication of concerns). Privacy & confidentiality (storage/protection, no sharing without consent except as legally required, FERPA framing — parent as rights-holder).

**4. Services agreement (pre-attorney draft).** Parties & scope (client = parent/guardian, possibly student named; you/your business entity; clarify confidentiality/decision-making client per Loyola Pro Bono Desk Manual guidance [1]; describe advocacy services; explicit non-legal-services statement; geography/jurisdiction limits). Term & termination (effective date, ongoing vs project-based, termination with notice 7-14 days, your termination grounds — non-payment, conflict, breakdown). Fees, billing, cancellation (hourly rate, flat fees, minimum billing increments e.g., 0.1 or 0.25 hour, retainer application/refund, invoice issuance/due, late payment consequences, meeting cancel/reschedule policy e.g., fee if <24h notice). Confidentiality & records (commitment to confidentiality, storage method, retention/disposal, parent authorization to review educational and medical/mental records, FERPA note — you are not a school official; parents share records directly; protect by contract and ethical practice). Communication & consent (may communicate with school/other professionals only with written consent; whether you communicate with student directly). Role clarification & no-guarantee clause (not attorney, no legal advice, no court/admin hearing representation, no outcome guarantees, reasonable professional efforts only). Limitation of liability & indemnification (reasonable liability cap where allowed, parent responsible for own decisions). Governing law & venue (state law specified, county/state for disputes).

**5. Signed agreement to first paid session.** Confirm engagement in writing (acknowledge signed agreement and payment, summarize next steps). Create client file & record system (secure digital folder; save agreement, intake form, initial documents). Send record request instructions (clear list, target date 5+ business days before first session). Schedule first paid session (60-90 minutes initial deep-dive — review story, clarify goals, begin reviewing IEP/evaluations, set initial strategy). Pre-session review billable (skim core documents, create brief outline/issues list). First session agenda (confirm understanding, align on top 3 priorities, agree on immediate plan — request assessments, draft parent input, prepare for upcoming IEP). After first session (short summary email with key points and action items; log time and update file).

**6. Common pitfalls.** Mismatched expectations (parents think you're a lawyer or can "force" the school — explicit role limits in discovery call, welcome packet, agreement, and verbal repeat in first paid session). Inadequate conflict screening (taken-on conflicting roles in small specialized programs — conflict questions in fit call and intake form, master client list to check). Poor FERPA/records handling (sensitive records via unsecured email or stored haphazardly — secure tools, written instructions, redaction where possible, internal access/retention/disposal policy). "Shadow legal advice" (drifting into legal analysis predicting hearing outcomes — frame as education, encourage legal counsel for due process / mediation / serious discipline). Taking crises without time (accept case with IEP/manifestation meeting tomorrow — hard policy on minimum lead time 3-5 business days, document exceptions and limited assistance). Lack of written process & boundaries (scope creep, constant unscheduled calls, burnout — put process and boundaries in writing, scheduling tools, gentle redirection).
