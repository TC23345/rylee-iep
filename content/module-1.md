## Naming & Registering Your Business Entity in Illinois

Illinois does not publicly require a Professional Limited Liability Company (PLLC) for BCBA-owned businesses — the Behavior Analyst Licensing Act (225 ILCS 6) speaks to licensure scope, not entity form, and ILABA's licensure FAQ likewise addresses when an LBA license is required but does not mandate a PLLC vs. LLC structure [1](https://ilaba.org/resources/licensure/). Because IEP advocacy is generally treated as non-clinical consulting rather than "behavior analysis services" under the Act, a standard single-member LLC is the most common and defensible choice for a non-clinical advocacy practice in Illinois [1](https://ilaba.org/resources/licensure/) [2](https://www.copaa.org/blogpost/895540/509774/How-to-Open-a-New-Special-Education-Law-or-Advocacy-Practice). The Illinois Secretary of State requires that the entity name be "distinguishable upon the records" from existing entities, include an LLC designator (e.g., "Limited Liability Company," "LLC," "L.L.C."), and avoid restricted terms (e.g., "Bank," "Insurance," "Clinic") unless qualified — availability can be checked at [apps.ilsos.gov/corporatellc](https://apps.ilsos.gov/corporatellc/) and optionally reserved for ~90 days [3](https://apps.ilsos.gov/). Filing Articles of Organization online via apps.ilsos.gov is typically processed within a few business days, while paper filings can take several weeks; the EIN is obtained separately and immediately from irs.gov [3](https://apps.ilsos.gov/) [4](https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/choose-business-structure). Sole proprietorship is the default if no entity is filed and offers no liability shield — for a professional giving advice that affects children's educational outcomes, the SBA and multiple business references recommend an LLC for asset protection, pass-through taxation, and a clearer business identity [4](https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business/choose-business-structure) [5](https://www.uschamber.com/co/start/strategy/sole-proprietorship-vs-llc). The BCBA credential itself does not restrict the entity name, but the practice description should be unambiguously advocacy/consulting (not "ABA clinic" or "treatment center") to avoid implying unlicensed clinical scope [1](https://ilaba.org/resources/licensure/) [2](https://www.copaa.org/blogpost/895540/509774/How-to-Open-a-New-Special-Education-Law-or-Advocacy-Practice).

## Insuring Your Private Practice: Liability Insurance

The standard BCBA professional-liability policies (CM&F, CPH & Associates, HPSO) are written around behavior-analytic "professional services" and may not automatically cover non-clinical educational advocacy — coverage depends on the declarations page's "professional capacity" and the policy's definition of professional services [1](https://cphins.com/board-certified-behavior-analyst-bcba/) [2](https://www.cmfgroup.com/blog/healthcare-professionals/the-hidden-costs-of-relying-on-employerprovided-insurance/) [3](https://www.cmfgroup.com/professional-liability-insurance/behavioral-health-professional-insurance/board-certified-behavior-analyst-bcba-insurance/). CPH and CM&F market BCBA policies with limits commonly at $1M per claim / $4M aggregate that explicitly extend to schools, clinics, in-home work, telehealth, and supervision — but advocacy as a distinct activity (records review, IEP meeting attendance, school observation, mediation support, expert opinion) often falls outside that scope unless endorsed in writing [1](https://cphins.com/board-certified-behavior-analyst-bcba/) [3](https://www.cmfgroup.com/professional-liability-insurance/behavioral-health-professional-insurance/board-certified-behavior-analyst-bcba-insurance/). There is no widely-marketed "IEP advocate" insurance line; coverage for special-education advocates is typically placed under miscellaneous professional liability / E&O programs via independent brokers, with carriers like The Hartford, Travelers, CNA, or Hiscox writing the risk under "educational consultant" classifications [4](https://www.thehartford.com/professional-liability-insurance) [5](https://www.hpso.com/Insurance-for-you/Individual-Practitioners/Other-healthcare-providers/Professions-covered). The scope-of-services description to underwriters should explicitly cover records review, non-attorney IEP/504 meeting participation, school observation with permission, mediation support, and written expert opinions — and should expressly disclaim legal advice and outcome guarantees [1](https://cphins.com/board-certified-behavior-analyst-bcba/) [4](https://www.thehartford.com/professional-liability-insurance). Typical premiums for BCBA-clinical policies run roughly $100–$300/year at base limits, while standalone E&O for a solo consultant tends toward $300–$800/year for $1M/$1M or $1M/$2M limits, with higher pricing if expert testimony is part of the scope [1](https://cphins.com/board-certified-behavior-analyst-bcba/) [3](https://www.cmfgroup.com/professional-liability-insurance/behavioral-health-professional-insurance/board-certified-behavior-analyst-bcba-insurance/). Employer-provided coverage from a public school district or cooperative almost never extends to outside private-pay work — independent confirmation in writing from the district's risk office is the only reliable way to know, and the default assumption should be "not covered" [2](https://www.cmfgroup.com/blog/healthcare-professionals/the-hidden-costs-of-relying-on-employerprovided-insurance/) [3](https://www.cmfgroup.com/professional-liability-insurance/behavioral-health-professional-insurance/board-certified-behavior-analyst-bcba-insurance/).

## Defining Your Services and Fee Structure

A solo IEP advocacy menu generally breaks into eight discrete line items: free discovery call, paid initial consultation, records review with written summary, IEP strategy/prep, IEP meeting attendance (with a 2-hour minimum to absorb travel and wait time), school observation, follow-up correspondence/drafting, mediation-or-due-process support, and BCBA-grounded expert review of FBAs/BIPs [1](https://www.copaa.org/page/GuidelinesAdv) [2](https://findparentadvocates.com/state/illinois). Pricing typically blends three models: hourly (the flexible baseline, billed in 0.1–0.25-hour increments), flat-fee packages for tightly-scoped deliverables like "IEP Review & Strategy" or "IEP Meeting Support," and retainers (either trust-style upfront balances or monthly access fees) for high-touch ongoing cases [1](https://www.copaa.org/page/GuidelinesAdv) [3](https://illinoisattorneygeneral.gov/Page-Attachments/Guidelines_ChoosingAdvocateSpecialEducation.pdf). Market hourly rates for independent IEP advocates in Illinois cluster around $100–$300/hour per parent-advocacy directories, with suburban Chicago/metro mid-level positioning around $125–$175/hour and experienced or dual-credentialed advocates reaching $175–$250/hour or more [2](https://findparentadvocates.com/state/illinois) [4](https://www.equipforequality.org/solutions/special-education-accommodations/). The BCBA credential meaningfully shifts pricing power because schools and parents recognize the clinical/educational credibility, especially in autism, behavior-plan, FBA/BIP, and restraint-and-seclusion cases — many BCBA-advocates run a tiered structure with a base advocacy rate (~$150–$185/hour) and a higher "expert behavior consultation" rate (~$185–$225/hour) for ABA-specific scope [2](https://findparentadvocates.com/state/illinois) [5](https://ilaba.org/resources/licensure/). Scope-of-engagement boundaries should be explicit and written into the services agreement: no legal representation or legal advice (the Illinois Attorney General's lay-advocate guidance distinguishes advocates from attorneys), no clinical diagnosis or ongoing ABA treatment under this engagement, no insurance billing (advocacy is private-pay only), and no guarantees of specific educational outcomes [3](https://illinoisattorneygeneral.gov/Page-Attachments/Guidelines_ChoosingAdvocateSpecialEducation.pdf) [5](https://ilaba.org/resources/licensure/). These exclusions protect Rylee from both BACB ethics issues (multiple-relationship and scope-creep risks) and Illinois IDFPR scope-of-practice concerns if advocacy ever drifted into something the licensure board would treat as clinical [3](https://illinoisattorneygeneral.gov/Page-Attachments/Guidelines_ChoosingAdvocateSpecialEducation.pdf).

## Backend Systems: File Organization and Contracts

A solo advocacy backend has to handle six jobs reliably: secure storage of student records, per-client file organization, contract signature and archival, scheduling, retention enforcement, and end-of-life deletion [1](https://www.recordnations.com/blog/ferpa-affect-student-records-storage/) [2](https://www.preveil.com/blog/email-file-sharing-ferpa-compliance/). FERPA does not prescribe a vendor — both Google Workspace Business and Microsoft 365 Business can be configured for sensitive student records, and the deciding factor is whether the operator can reliably enforce MFA, restricted external sharing, role-based access, audit logs, and folder-level permissions [1](https://www.recordnations.com/blog/ferpa-affect-student-records-storage/) [3](https://aws.amazon.com/compliance/ferpa/). The recommended folder pattern is a single business root with a per-client folder (Lastname_Firstname or `<client_id>`) subdivided into intake/consent, evaluations, IEP/504 documents, school communications, meeting notes, action items, drafts, final deliverables, and invoice/contract, plus a date-prefixed naming convention like `YYYY-MM-DD_DocumentType_ClientName_Version` for fast retrieval and audit [3](https://aws.amazon.com/compliance/ferpa/) [4](https://www.wrightslaw.com/info/organize.file.htm). Retention norms for advocacy records typically use a 7-year baseline after case closure — FERPA itself sets no fixed retention period, so the 7-year figure derives from civil-record, contract-dispute, and tax norms, with longer holds applied when state law, contract terms, an anticipated dispute, or a legal hold require it [1](https://www.recordnations.com/blog/ferpa-affect-student-records-storage/) [3](https://aws.amazon.com/compliance/ferpa/). For e-signature on the services agreement, dedicated tools (Dropbox Sign / HelloSign, DocuSign, PandaDoc) are recommended over Google's lightweight native signer because they provide stronger audit trails, reusable templates, automatic reminders, and clean PDF archival — Dropbox Sign and DocuSign are the simplest fits for a solo practice, while PandaDoc adds proposal-style polish [2](https://www.preveil.com/blog/email-file-sharing-ferpa-compliance/) [5](https://calendly.com). Scheduling should be the front door of intake: Calendly or Google Appointments collects a minimal intake snippet, triggers an automatic welcome email, then routes the client to the services agreement and secure document upload — bookings should not be allowed before contract signature if policy requires it [5](https://calendly.com). Secure deletion at end-of-retention requires more than emptying trash: duplicates in synced folders, email attachments, exports, and backup cycles all need attention, and a written deletion log (client, closure date, retention end, deletion date, locations cleared) provides defensibility [3](https://aws.amazon.com/compliance/ferpa/) [4](https://www.wrightslaw.com/info/organize.file.htm).

## Your Custom Client Intake Process

The standard solo-advocate intake flow runs inquiry → discovery/fit call → intake form + document request → welcome packet + services agreement → signature & payment → records review → first paid session, with each step having a defined gate before the next [1](https://www.copaa.org/page/GuidelinesAdv) [2](https://probonodeskmanual.loyno.edu/book/export/html/659). The discovery call is typically 20–30 minutes, free, by phone or secure video, and serves three purposes: confirm scope fit (issue type, jurisdiction, complexity, timeline), screen for advocate-side conflicts of interest (prior representation of the district, dual relationships in small specialized programs), and set realistic expectations about what an advocate can and cannot do [1](https://www.copaa.org/page/GuidelinesAdv) [3](https://www.disabilityrightsca.org/publications/17-special-education-advocacy-tips). The intake form should collect family contact info, student basics (grade, school, district, primary language), top 1–3 concerns, current IEP/504/evaluation status, upcoming meeting or hearing dates, a checklist of records to gather (last 1–2 IEPs, evaluations, progress reports, BIP, discipline records), and a "how did you hear about me" referral source — and it must be collected via secure form/upload, not email attachments [1](https://www.copaa.org/page/GuidelinesAdv) [4](https://sierraspecialeducationadvocacy.com/client-forms). The welcome packet sets expectations in writing: what you do (records review, IEP/504 attendance as a non-attorney advocate, parent-input drafting, behavior-plan review), what you do not do (legal representation, clinical treatment, outcome guarantees, direct school communication without consent), communication norms (channels, response times, after-hours rules), and high-level fees and billing cadence [3](https://www.disabilityrightsca.org/publications/17-special-education-advocacy-tips) [4](https://sierraspecialeducationadvocacy.com/client-forms). The services agreement — before sending to an attorney for review — should already cover parties, scope of services, term and termination, fees and billing including cancellation and minimum increments, confidentiality and FERPA-aware records handling, communication consent, an explicit non-attorney/non-clinical role clarification with no-guarantee clause, limitation of liability, and governing law (Illinois) [2](https://probonodeskmanual.loyno.edu/book/export/html/659) [5](https://disabilityrightsnj.org/wp-content/uploads/New-Jersey-Special-Education-Advocacy-Guide.pdf). From signed agreement to first paid session, the operational steps are: confirm engagement in writing, create the secure client file, send specific record-request instructions with a target date 5+ business days before the first session, schedule a 60–90-minute initial deep-dive, perform billable pre-session review, and follow up with a written summary and action items [1](https://www.copaa.org/page/GuidelinesAdv) [2](https://probonodeskmanual.loyno.edu/book/export/html/659). The most common intake pitfalls are mismatched expectations (parents thinking the advocate functions as a lawyer), inadequate conflict screening, unsecured records handling, drift into "shadow legal advice," accepting crisis cases without sufficient lead time, and absence of written boundaries — all preventable with disciplined process and clear written documents [3](https://www.disabilityrightsca.org/publications/17-special-education-advocacy-tips) [5](https://disabilityrightsnj.org/wp-content/uploads/New-Jersey-Special-Education-Advocacy-Guide.pdf).
