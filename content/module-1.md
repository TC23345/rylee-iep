## Naming & Registering Your Business Entity in Illinois

### Initial research context

For Illinois, the entity decision is not just "LLC or sole proprietor"; because Rylee is a licensed BCBA, the first checkpoint is whether the planned business will hold itself out as providing behavior analysis services, IEP advocacy services, or both. Illinois' Behavior Analyst Licensing Act says a business whose stated purpose includes behavior analysis, or that holds itself out as practicing behavior analysis therapy, must be organized under the Professional Service Corporation Act or Professional Limited Liability Company Act; that is an IDFPR/attorney verification item before filing. If the business is framed as private-pay IEP advocacy rather than ABA therapy, the name, website copy, service menu, and client agreement should still avoid accidentally implying a regulated ABA service line unless Rylee intentionally wants that structure reviewed.

The Illinois Secretary of State's LLC filing flow requires the LLC name to include "Limited Liability Company," "L.L.C.," or "LLC," bars corporate terms like "Corporation" or "Inc.," and requires the name to be distinguishable from other LLC/corporate records. Name research should therefore have three layers: Illinois Secretary of State availability, USPTO trademark knock-out searching for similar education/advocacy service marks, and a practical web/domain/social search. After the state entity is formed, the IRS says to form the state legal entity before applying for an EIN, and Illinois Department of Revenue registration runs through MyTax Illinois/Form REG-1 when the business has Illinois tax obligations.

### Notes for Rylee

- Decide whether the public-facing offer is "IEP advocacy/education consulting," "behavior analysis," or a deliberately separated menu. This affects entity type, insurance language, and website copy.
- Ask IDFPR and an Illinois attorney whether an Illinois BCBA-owned IEP advocacy practice should file as LLC, PLLC, or another professional entity if the business does not provide ABA therapy.
- Keep the legal name and public brand name separate if needed: a legally compliant LLC/PLLC name can be boring while the DBA/brand carries the parent-facing message.
- Do not store EIN or filing numbers in the repo; track only "obtained / pending" in the business checklist.

## Insuring Your Private Practice: Liability Insurance

### Initial research context

The insurance question should be treated as a coverage-matching exercise, not just "add a rider." SBA's small-business guidance separates general liability from professional liability: general liability protects against things like bodily injury/property damage and related lawsuits, while professional liability protects service businesses against financial loss tied to malpractice, errors, or negligence. For an IEP advocacy practice, the most relevant coverage conversation is likely professional liability/errors and omissions for advice, record review, meeting participation, written recommendations, and parent coaching, plus general liability if Rylee meets families in person or rents space.

Because Rylee is licensed as a BCBA in Illinois, the key practical question is whether her existing BCBA professional liability policy covers non-clinical advocacy/consulting, excludes it, or requires a separate business policy. The Illinois Behavior Analyst Licensing Act and IDFPR notice make it especially important that the policy application and service description match what the business actually does. If the practice is not billing insurance and is private-pay only, the insurance file should still include written confirmation of covered services, entity name/DBA, coverage limits, retroactive date if claims-made, and whether document review and meeting attendance are included.

### Notes for Rylee

- Send the carrier/broker a plain-English service description: private-pay IEP advocacy, parent consultation, document review, meeting preparation/attendance, written meeting summaries, and no insurance billing.
- Ask whether coverage applies to the entity, Rylee individually, contractors/helpers, virtual meetings, in-person school meetings, written summaries, and prior work.
- Ask whether the BCBA credential creates exclusions, special underwriting, or a need to separate "advocacy" from "clinical ABA."
- Keep carrier quotes and declarations in local/private business files, not committed repo files.

## Defining Your Services and Fee Structure

### Initial research context

Rylee's strongest starting position is a narrowly defined, private-pay service ladder that converts her school-side BCBA/IEP fluency into advocate-side deliverables without promising legal outcomes. IDEA regulations allow parents or the school agency to invite other people with knowledge or special expertise about the child to the IEP team, and the inviting party determines that expertise. That gives a clean role frame: Rylee can help parents organize records, clarify concerns, prepare meeting priorities, understand draft IEP language, participate as a knowledgeable invited support, and document follow-up questions.

Pricing research shows wide variation in advocate fees, with common models including free/low-cost consults, flat-fee document review, hourly meeting preparation/attendance, and higher-touch packages for ongoing support. The plan should avoid overbuilding too many packages at launch. A practical first draft is: one strategy consult, one records/IEP review product, one meeting-prep-plus-attendance package, and hourly follow-up for work outside scope. The service agreement should make clear that Rylee is not acting as an attorney, is not guaranteeing school decisions, and is not providing clinical treatment unless that service line is separately reviewed.

### Notes for Rylee

- Start with services that map to concrete parent outcomes: "know what to ask," "understand what the IEP says," "walk into the meeting organized," and "leave with a written next-step list."
- Separate included work from out-of-scope work: document volume, email time, travel, meeting length, emergency calls, dispute escalation, and post-meeting summaries.
- Consider a launch menu with three simple options: consult, IEP/records review, and meeting support package.
- Route due process strategy, legal claims, compensatory education legal analysis, contract interpretation, and settlement questions to an attorney.

## Backend Systems: File Organization and Contracts

### Initial research context

The backend system should be designed around privacy, retrieval, and scope control. FERPA directly governs schools and education agencies, not every private consultant in every context, but the records Rylee will handle are still sensitive education and disability records. The U.S. Department of Education states that consent to disclose education records must be signed and dated, identify the records, state the purpose, and identify the receiving party or class of parties. For Rylee, that means the intake workflow should include a parent authorization/release process before requesting records from a district, and the file system should separate lead metadata from actual student records.

Contracts are a legal drafting area, so Codex should not generate binding terms as final advice. The operational checklist can still define what the attorney should review: scope of services, fees, payment timing, cancellation/no-show rules, confidentiality, record handling, communication boundaries, role disclaimer, no-guarantee language, escalation/referral triggers, and termination. The backend should support the contract rather than fight it: every client folder should have intake, signed agreement, consent/release, records received, meeting notes, action items, invoices, and closure/archive.

### Notes for Rylee

- Use a two-tier data model: client index metadata in the app; sensitive client records in private storage with strict access controls.
- Build a standard folder template before the first real client: intake, agreement, releases, records, notes, correspondence, invoices, closure.
- Keep the services agreement as an attorney-reviewed document; the dashboard can track checklist status but should not become the legal source of truth.
- Add a retention decision to the attorney checklist: how long records are kept, how clients request copies, and how closure/destruction is documented.

## Your Custom Client Intake Process

### Initial research context

The intake process should convert a parent's messy story into a bounded advocacy matter while minimizing unnecessary PII collection. A good first version can collect only what is needed to triage: parent goals, immediate deadlines, current eligibility/services, recent evaluations/IEP dates, district abbreviation, meeting status, and whether a legal/dispute deadline is already active. The app's current `client_index` model intentionally stores only metadata, which is the right v1 posture until encryption, consent, retention, and attorney-review decisions are complete.

Because parents may invite advocates or other knowledgeable people to an IEP meeting, intake should ask what role Rylee is being asked to play: parent coach outside the meeting, document reviewer, meeting attendee, follow-up organizer, or referral connector. It should also identify boundary triggers early: requests for legal advice, due process strategy, contract disputes, clinical diagnoses, independent clinical recommendations for a child Rylee has not assessed, and any request to bill insurance. Those items should route to the right professional rather than becoming hidden scope creep.

### Notes for Rylee

- Intake should end with a decision: accept, decline, refer out, or request more information.
- The first form should avoid full records upload until the client has signed the services agreement and consent/release.
- Create a "red flag" section for urgent meetings, expired timelines, active complaints/due process, safety concerns, and attorney involvement.
- Save form submissions only after the data-security pass; until then, keep the production app metadata-only and use private storage for real client records.
